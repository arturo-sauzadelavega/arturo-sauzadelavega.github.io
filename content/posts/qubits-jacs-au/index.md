#### Abstract

> Pseudotetrahedral Cr(IV) aryl complexes have recently emerged as promising candidates for molecular spin qubits. In this work, we present a robust computational protocol based on multiconfiguration pair-density functional theory (MC-PDFT) to evaluate singlet–triplet gaps and zero-field splitting (ZFS) parameters in these systems. Our results demonstrate that multireference approaches—specifically complete active space second-order perturbation theory (CASPT2) and hybrid multiconfiguration pair-density functional theory (HMC-PDFT)—outperform standard Kohn-Sham density functional theory (KS-DFT) in predicting singlet-triplet energy gaps. Despite the inherently small magnitude of the ZFS parameters, both multireference methods provide high qualitative accuracy, particularly in capturing the trend of the rhombic-to-axial ratio, *|E/D|*. Furthermore, we examine the sensitivity of ZFS predictions to active space selection and structural deviations. The methodologies established herein provide a reliable framework for the rational design and screening of future molecular spin qubit candidates.

#### Key Ideas

> - **Structural Precision**: Molecular geometry is critical for the accurate reproduction of Zero-Field Splitting (ZFS) parameters in the GHz regime, where even minor distortions significantly impact the electronic manifold.
> - **Active Space Convergence**: To achieve a semi-quantitative description of both axial (*|D|*) and rhombic (*|E|*) parameters, the use of expanded active spaces consistently yields superior results for both CASPT2 and MC-PDFT frameworks.
> - **Energy Gap Accuracy**: The hybrid MC-PDFT functional effectively captures the singlet–triplet energy gaps across all studied complexes, proving its reliability for describing the low-lying spin states of Cr(IV) systems.
> - **Methodological Consistency**: Both MS-CASPT2 and hybrid MC-PDFT recover consistent semi-quantitative trends for the axial parameter and the rhombic-to-axial ratio (*|E/D|*), validating their use for the rational screening of molecular qubits.
